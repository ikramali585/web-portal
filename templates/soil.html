Soil Texture

Since it is concerned with the size of mineral particles, soil texture refers to the relative percentage of sand, silt, and clay. The inorganic solid phase of the soil is composed primarily of sand, silt, and clay particles. These particles frequently combine with other soil constituents, most significantly soil organic matter. Cultural actions do not readily alter the particle size of mineral soil. As a result, it is regarded as a constant aspect and fundamental property of soil.

Classification

The United States Department of Agriculture has twelve main classifications for soil texture. These are sand, loamy sand, sandy loam,loam, silt loam, silt, sandy clay loam, clay loam, silty clay loam, sandy clay, silty clay, and clay.The proportions of each soil component (sand, silt, and clay) that are present in a soil are used to categorise its texture.The use of a soil texture triangle is connected to determining soil texture. Below is an example of a soil triangle:The triangle's first side represents the percentage of sand, the second side the percentage of clay, and the third side the percentage of silt. The triangle can be used to classify the soil texture if the amounts of sand, clay, and silt in the soil sample are known.
For example, a soil is categorised as a sandy loam if it contains 70% sand and 10% clay. Starting on any side of the soil triangle will get the same results. The physical and chemical characteristics of a soil affect its texture. The amount of water and nutrients that a soil can contain will depend on the size and distribution of its particles. Sandier soils have huge pore spaces that enable leaching, whereas fine-textured soils typically have a higher capacity for water retention.
